Berkeley: Exposed

Computer Science students capture graphic scenes from around the college campus in stunning exposé

All Was Captured With A Pinhole Camera

   The photographers, Steven Traversi and Ross Teixeira, built their pinhole camera using a cardboard box, duct and electical tape, construction paper, felt, and foam. The inside of the cardboard box was lined with the felt, which rounded the corners nicely. The pinhole itself was pierced into the construction paper. The felt was used to drape over the closed box to ensure no unwanted light entered through undetected cracks. The duct tape held everything together, and the electrical tape was used inside of the box because of its flat coloration (to avoid glare). Two constructions were tried; one where the camera (in this case, iPhone) was placed on top of the box and looked down onto a paper resting at a 45 degree angle; the second where the camera looked through the box from the side. Both constructions are pictured to the right. The photographers decided to use the second model after some trial and error. An example advantage of the second model is that the image doesn't need to be transformed after.

Exclusive: Different Aperatures Tested

Traversi and Teixeira reportedly first tested their pinhole camera with three separate pinhole radii. Comp Photo Times was granted an exclusive on six early photos taken during Traversi and Teixeira's testing phase. At the end of the period, they found that the images grew brighter, yet generally duller as the size of the pinhole increased. They agreed on the 2.5mm pinhole as the optimal combination of the two, and used it for the rest of their photos. Their first set of photos is pictured below. The scene in question is Doe Library at UC Berkeley.
